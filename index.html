Mary Teresa Bojaxhiu MC

1910--1997

Mother Teresa

Mother Teresa, originally named Agnes Gonxha Bojaxhiu, was born on August 26, 1910, in Skopje, Macedonia. From a young age, she felt a deep calling to help others, leading her to join the Sisters of Loreto at 18. She was sent to India, where she spent years teaching before experiencing a "call within a call" to serve the poorest of the poor. In 1950, she founded the Missionaries of Charity in Calcutta, an order dedicated to caring for those society had forgotten: the sick, the orphaned, and the dying. Her work spread across the globe, establishing homes for the destitute and sick in over 130 countries. Mother Teresa's life was a testament to selfless service and unwavering faith, earning her the Nobel Peace Prize in 1979 and global admiration. Despite facing criticism, her dedication to humanity's most vulnerable never wavered. She passed away on September 5, 1997, and was canonized as Saint Teresa of Calcutta in 2016, continuing to inspire countless individuals with her message of love, compassion, and humility.

Achievements

  • Founded the Missionaries of Charity (1950): Established a religious congregation in Calcutta, India, dedicated to helping the "poorest of the poor."
  • Expanded Missionaries of Charity Globally: Grew the organization to over 130 countries, with thousands of members working in hospitals, orphanages, and homes for the dying.
  • Nobel Peace Prize (1979): Awarded for her unwavering dedication to helping the poor and her role in promoting peace through humanitarian work.
  • Padma Shri Award (1962): Received one of India's highest civilian honors for her service to the nation.
  • Bharat Ratna (1980): India's highest civilian award, recognizing her exceptional contribution to society.
  • Order of Merit (1983): Honored by Queen Elizabeth II, becoming one of the few recipients outside the Commonwealth.
  • Templeton Prize (1973): Awarded for her work in spiritual leadership.
  • Canonization as Saint Teresa of Calcutta (2016): Recognized as a saint by the Catholic Church, highlighting her life's work and enduring legacy.
  • Established Homes for the Dying: Created hospices for the terminally ill in India and beyond, offering care and dignity to those in their final days.
  • Advocated for the Voiceless: Spoke out against poverty, abortion, and violence, becoming a global symbol of compassion and moral integrity.
